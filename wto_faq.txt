Q: What is the World Trade Organization?
A: The World Trade Organization (WTO) is an international organization that deals with the global rules of trade between nations, ensuring trade flows smoothly, predictably, and freely.

Q: What are the main functions of the WTO?
A: The WTO administers trade agreements, acts as a forum for trade negotiations, settles trade disputes, monitors national trade policies, and provides technical assistance to developing countries.

Q: What is a trade dispute?
A: A trade dispute arises when one WTO member believes another is violating a WTO agreement, resolved through the WTO's Dispute Settlement Body.

Q: What are tariffs?
A: Tariffs are taxes imposed by governments on imported or exported goods to regulate trade or generate revenue.

Q: What is the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT)?
A: GATT is a multilateral agreement aimed at reducing trade barriers, which was replaced by the WTO in 1995 but remains a core WTO agreement.

Q: What is the Doha Development Agenda?
A: The Doha Development Agenda is a WTO trade negotiation round launched in 2001 to address trade issues, particularly for developing countries.

Q: What are non-tariff measures (NTMs)?
A: Non-tariff measures are trade barriers other than tariffs, such as quotas, import licensing, or technical standards, that affect trade flows.

Q: How does the WTO support developing countries?
A: The WTO provides technical assistance, training, and special provisions like longer transition periods to help developing countries comply with trade rules.

Q: What is the Trade Facilitation Agreement?
A: The Trade Facilitation Agreement is a WTO agreement to simplify customs procedures, reduce trade costs, and enhance efficiency in global trade.

Q: What is the role of the WTO Secretariat?
A: The WTO Secretariat, based in Geneva, supports member governments by providing technical expertise, facilitating negotiations, and administering WTO activities.
